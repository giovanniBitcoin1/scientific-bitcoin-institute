Strategy Inc. — the company once known as MicroStrategy (Nasdaq:
**MSTR**) — is the largest corporate holder of Bitcoin on earth. But the
most interesting thing about it isn't the size of the pile. It's that a
mid-sized software firm has built, in plain sight, an entire layer of
**financial infrastructure** around Bitcoin: common stock, preferred
shares, and convertible bonds engineered to raise capital, accumulate
Bitcoin, and grow the amount of Bitcoin behind every share. In doing so
it has quietly handed Bitcoin exposure to people who would never open a
crypto wallet.

That is the through-line of this explainer. We cover what the machine is
and why someone would buy a share of it instead of buying Bitcoin; how
the accretion flywheel works; where the dividend cash comes from; why
the "Ponzi" label doesn't hold up legally; and — the heart of the
bullish case — why building real financial plumbing around Bitcoin is a
genuine maturation event that expands the universe of buyers. We then
examine the long-run trajectory through the Bitcoin power law, drawing
on the Scientific Bitcoin Institute's own research (a 2026 preprint),
show why the structure's biggest risk fades with time, and finish with
references and a glossary. Charts illustrate the mechanism and the
trend.

*Nothing here is financial advice. Every figure is illustrative and
reflects conditions as of mid-2026; prices, rates, and balance-sheet
details change constantly.*

## 1. What Strategy actually is

Underneath the Bitcoin story, Strategy still runs a legacy
business-analytics software operation. But its identity today is a
financial structure: a single Bitcoin reserve financed by a deliberately
layered "capital stack." Each layer is a different security aimed at a
different kind of investor, in order of who gets paid first if the
company is ever wound down.

From safest (paid first) to riskiest (paid last):

| **Layer**         | **What it is**                                                         | **Risk / reward**                 |
|-------------------|------------------------------------------------------------------------|-----------------------------------|
| Convertible notes | Corporate debt, often near-0% interest, convertible into MSTR shares   | Safest; first in line             |
| STRF ("Strife")   | Senior perpetual preferred, ~10% cumulative dividend, cash             | Conservative income               |
| STRK ("Strike")   | Convertible preferred; ~8% plus equity upside if MSTR soars            | Income + option                   |
| STRC ("Stretch")  | Variable-rate perpetual preferred; rate adjusts monthly to hold ~\$100 | Bond-like, money-market style     |
| STRD ("Stride")   | Junior preferred, ~10% non-cumulative dividend                         | Higher yield, higher risk         |
| MSTR common       | Ordinary ownership stock; votes; unlimited upside                      | Last in line; highest risk/reward |

The whole arrangement is what people mean by a **Bitcoin-backed yield
curve**: one underlying asset, sliced into instruments that trade safety
against upside — a structure that looks a lot like the bond-and-equity
stack of any mature company, only with Bitcoin as the reserve asset.

## 2. Why buy MSTR instead of just buying Bitcoin?

If you simply want Bitcoin exposure, you can buy Bitcoin. So why own a
company that holds it? Three reasons:

1.  Leverage. Strategy uses debt and preferred stock to control more
    Bitcoin than its own cash could, so the stock has historically moved
    more than Bitcoin in both directions — at times several times as
    much. Amplified upside is the appeal.

2.  The accretion flywheel. When the stock trades above the value of its
    Bitcoin, the company can issue shares at that premium, buy more
    Bitcoin, and raise the Bitcoin-per-share that existing holders own —
    something no passive vehicle can do.

3.  Access and convenience. MSTR sits in any ordinary brokerage or
    retirement account, no exchange, wallet, or private keys required —
    plus a deep options market on top.

The honest framing: buying MSTR is Bitcoin *multiplied* by leverage, by
a premium multiple, and by management's capital-markets execution. When
those align, it can outpace Bitcoin handsomely; when they reverse, it
falls faster. That second half is the risk — and, as we'll see, it is
the part that shrinks as Bitcoin grows.

## 3. The flywheel, with numbers

The flywheel only works at a premium. Here is the whole mechanism in one
example. Start with a simple company: 100 shares, 100 BTC, Bitcoin at
\$100,000 — so Bitcoin-per-share is 1.0. The company issues 10 new
shares and spends every dollar on Bitcoin. What happens to
Bitcoin-per-share depends entirely on the price the stock fetches when
it sells.

| **Scenario**        | **Stock price** | **Cash raised → BTC bought** | **New BTC / share** | **Result**  |
|---------------------|-----------------|------------------------------|---------------------|-------------|
| Premium (mNAV 2.0)  | \$200,000       | \$2.0M → 20 BTC              | 120 / 110 = 1.09    | Accretive ↑ |
| At NAV (mNAV 1.0)   | \$100,000       | \$1.0M → 10 BTC              | 110 / 110 = 1.00    | Neutral →   |
| Discount (mNAV 0.8) | \$80,000        | \$0.8M → 8 BTC               | 108 / 110 = 0.98    | Dilutive ↓  |

Selling *overvalued* stock to buy Bitcoin is the magic — and it only
works while the stock trades above its Bitcoin. This is what Michael
Saylor meant at BTC Prague 2026 when he told Jack Mallers, *"The idea
that selling equity is dilutive is a misnomer"*: above NAV, each raise
genuinely grows Bitcoin-per-share. The discipline the model demands is
to issue when the premium is fat and lean on lower-cost capital when it
isn't.

## 4. Where the dividend money comes from

The preferred dividends are large and fixed in dollars — on the order of
a billion dollars a year across the stack — while the legacy software
business throws off only around \$100 million. The gap is covered, in
order of importance, by:

- New capital raises — the dominant source. At-the-market sales of MSTR
  common and of more preferred shares. For STRC in particular, the same
  raise funds both the Bitcoin purchase and the dividend.

- A multi-billion-dollar USD cash reserve, built specifically to cover
  preferred dividends and interest.

- Selling a sliver of Bitcoin when needed — a small first sale was
  disclosed in mid-2026 to fund preferred dividends.

Critics call the circularity "Ponzi-like." But notice the bullish
counterpoint that the rest of this article develops: as Bitcoin
appreciates, that billion-dollar obligation becomes a **rounding error**
against a multi-hundred-billion-dollar reserve, and the dependence on
fresh capital fades. The funding question is acute today and trivial
later.

## 5. "Is it a Ponzi?" — and why regulators allow it

A Ponzi scheme is defined by **fraud and concealment**, not by funding
distributions with new money (closed-end funds and others do versions of
that legally). By the legal test, Strategy is not a Ponzi: it holds
hundreds of thousands of *real, audited* Bitcoin that can appreciate on
their own; it discloses everything in SEC filings and audited
statements; and its dividends are board-declared and conditional, not
fabricated guarantees.

Regulators "allow" it because U.S. securities law is disclosure-based,
not merit-based: the SEC ensures investors get complete, accurate
information, then lets them decide. Every building block — common stock,
preferred stock, convertibles, at-the-market offerings — is a
decades-old, perfectly legal instrument. The Ponzi comparison captures a
real solvency question, but mislabels it as fraud, which — given the
disclosure and audits — is precisely what it is not.

## 6. The bigger picture: building financial infrastructure around Bitcoin

Step back from any single ticker and the more durable story comes into
view. What Strategy and its imitators are really doing is wrapping
Bitcoin in the familiar machinery of traditional finance — equity,
credit, dividends, ratings, disclosure — and that wrapping is itself a
maturation event with real benefits.

### Why the infrastructure itself matters

For most of its life Bitcoin offered exactly one thing: price exposure.
No coupon, no yield, no fixed-income profile — just a volatile spot
price you had to custody yourself. The arrival of regulated custody,
institutional-grade trading, and standardized reporting has steadily
removed the operational barriers that once kept large allocators out. On
top of that plumbing, instruments like Strategy's preferreds begin to do
something new: they turn a non-yielding asset into a source of income.
Analysts have gone so far as to suggest that a maturing "Bitcoin yield"
could eventually function as a parallel reference rate alongside the
Treasury curve — a sign of an asset moving from speculation toward a
standard portfolio building block.

### Reaching the people who will never touch Bitcoin

This is the crux of the bull case, and it is often missed. A huge pool
of capital is structurally unable or unwilling to hold Bitcoin directly:
retirees and income funds that need a yield, conservative mandates that
forbid commodities or crypto but permit equities and preferred stock,
institutions that can't self-custody, and ordinary savers who find
wallets and private keys intimidating. A dividend-paying,
exchange-listed preferred share backed by Bitcoin meets all of those
investors where they already are.

The evidence that this expands the audience is concrete. Strategy's
management has noted that its Stretch (STRC) preferred is held
overwhelmingly by retail investors — a base distinct from the largely
institutional holders of MSTR common — so the company captures two
different kinds of demand without one cannibalizing the other. Across
the sector, treasury companies have raised billions in high-yield
preferred specifically to court fixed-income investors who want regular
income rather than direct exposure to a volatile coin. Bitcoin's own
co-architect, Adam Back, has argued that these recurring, structured
buyers, layered on top of new institutional and wealth-management
demand, will in time overwhelm the sellers.

The strategic point: every dollar that reaches Bitcoin through a
preferred share, an ETF, or a treasury company is a dollar that would
otherwise have stayed on the sidelines. Wrapping Bitcoin in instruments
people already understand **grows the total addressable market** for the
asset — a demand tailwind that compounds as the infrastructure deepens.

### How this differs from a spot Bitcoin ETF

Spot ETFs (BlackRock's IBIT, Fidelity's FBTC, and peers) were a landmark
in their own right, and for many investors they are the right tool. But
they and treasury companies are different instruments, not competitors
for the same job. The table makes the contrast concrete.

| **Feature**       | **Spot Bitcoin ETF**                              | **Treasury company (e.g., MSTR)**                       |
|-------------------|---------------------------------------------------|---------------------------------------------------------|
| Exposure          | Clean ~1:1 to spot Bitcoin                        | Leveraged, amplified — can move 2–5x                    |
| Price vs. NAV     | Trades near NAV via creation/redemption arbitrage | Premium or discount, set by sentiment (no redemption)   |
| Bitcoin per share | Fixed — cannot grow                               | Can grow over time via accretion ("BTC Yield")          |
| Income / yield    | None                                              | A whole menu of dividend-paying preferreds              |
| Cost              | Low expense ratio (~0.12–0.25%)                   | No direct fee; cost is premium + dilution + credit risk |
| What it is        | A passive fund holding coins                      | An operating company with a capital structure           |

The decisive difference for the access story is **income**. An ETF can
only ever hand you Bitcoin's price; it cannot pay a Bitcoin-backed
dividend. A treasury company can — and that single feature unlocks the
income-oriented and conservative capital an ETF cannot reach. In
practice the two are complements: a common framework pairs a low-fee ETF
core for clean exposure with a smaller treasury-company satellite for
amplified upside and yield. Together they widen the on-ramps to Bitcoin
far more than either could alone.

## 7. The long view: the Bitcoin power law

To think about where this leads over decades, we need a model of
Bitcoin's long-run price. The most rigorously tested one is the
**Bitcoin power law**, which holds that price grows as a power of time,
P(t) ∝ t^β. This is not a casual chart-fitting exercise: in a Scientific
Bitcoin Institute preprint ([Santostasi & Perrenod, 2026](https://doi.org/10.5281/zenodo.19386895),
available on Zenodo), the exponent β = 5.69 ± 0.05 is measured across 5,696 daily
observations spanning 2010–2026, with the log-residuals stationary and
showing no secular drift over fifteen years.

### Why the exponent isn't arbitrary

The central scientific result is that the exponent is derived rather
than fitted. It factorises into two independently established
mechanisms: how fast the network adds users, and how value scales with
users.

![Chart 1 — The exponent is a product of two known scaling laws, not a free parameter.](images/chart_decomposition.png "Chart 1 — The exponent is a product of two known scaling laws, not a free parameter.")

The first factor, **βₐ ≈ 3.05**, is the adoption exponent: the count of
active Bitcoin addresses grows as roughly t³. That cubic growth is the
signature of epidemic spreading on a heterogeneous, scale-free network —
the same mathematics that produced cubic growth in the early AIDS
epidemic, where a "saturation wave" moves from highly-connected hubs out
to less-connected tiers. The second factor, **βₘ ≈ 1.84**, is a
generalised Metcalfe's law: a network's value scales with about the 1.84
power of its user count (between linear and the classic N²). Their
product, 3.05 × 1.84 ≈ 5.60, matches the directly measured 5.69 to
within 1.6%.

### Why it's a scaling law, not just a straight line

A fair objection to any log-log fit is that taking logarithms can make
almost any rising series look linear. The paper meets that objection
head-on with tests that a mere fit would fail. A multi-asset comparison
applies the identical method to Bitcoin, the NASDAQ, the S&P 500, and
gold: Bitcoin alone shows true scale invariance (no preferred
timescale), while the others curve — steep over short horizons, flat
over long ones. A direct "collapse" test built from thousands of price
ratios (which are immune to any shared trend) recovers the same
exponent, 5.59. Formal econometrics agree: stationarity tests confirm
the relationship is trend-stationary rather than spurious, and a model
fitted only through 2020 predicts 2021–2026 with every out-of-sample
point landing inside its expected band.

Crucially, this does not deny Bitcoin's wild cycles. In this framework
the bull-and-bust swings are *mean-reverting deviations around a
long-run attractor* — oscillations of roughly ±0.30 dex (about a factor
of two either way), correlated with the four-year halving cycle. The
power law is the spine; speculation is the wobble around it.

### The trajectory and what it means for Strategy

![Chart 2 — The power-law trajectory on the paper's own fit, with the cyclical band. Markers show the central line.](images/chart_powerlaw.png "Chart 2 — The power-law trajectory on the paper's own fit, with the cyclical band. Markers show the central line.")

Extended forward on the paper's own coefficients, the central trajectory
runs from roughly six figures today toward the multi-million-dollar
range by the mid-2040s — on the order of \$8–10 million per coin — with
the shaded band reminding us that any given year can sit a factor of two
above or below the line. Run that through the Strategy machine and the
implication is stark: the fixed, dollar-denominated obligations that
look heavy today become negligible against a reserve heading toward the
hundreds of billions and then trillions. The preferreds become
rock-solid and the solvency critique dissolves.

One nuance keeps the analysis honest: as Bitcoin matures along this
trend, the *premium* that supercharges MSTR common can compress, so the
leveraged equity may not capture the full multiple even as the coins do.
The bonds and preferreds, by contrast, simply get safer. A balanced way
to express a power-law thesis is therefore to hold Bitcoin (or a spot
ETF) for the clean upside and Bitcoin-backed preferreds for durable,
increasingly well-covered income.

### The cohort cascade: how treasury companies feed the law

Here the two halves of this article meet. Among the economic feedback
loops the paper offers as candidate drivers of the trend is a **cohort
cascade**: early individual holders progressively sell into demand from
larger, better-capitalised entrants — first institutions and corporate
treasuries, more recently spot ETFs and even sovereigns. Because each
successive tier commands greater resources, the marginal coin changes
hands at a steadily higher price, and that rising price draws in the
next, larger class of buyer.

Strategy and its peers are not separate from the power law in this
picture — they are part of its machinery. The financial infrastructure
of Section 6 (dividend-paying preferreds, listed wrappers, credit
instruments) widens the cascade by pulling in income-oriented and
conservative capital that would never hold a coin directly. More
participants feed the Metcalfe channel, P ∝ N^1.84, which feeds price.
The "access" story and the "scaling law" story are the same phenomenon
seen from two sides: building plumbing around Bitcoin is one of the
mechanisms by which the network's value compounds.

## 8. The risk that fades with time

The structure's real vulnerability has never been the long-run average —
it is surviving the troughs along the way. The same research that
establishes the power-law trend also quantifies the cycles around it:
deviations of roughly ±0.30 dex, a factor of about two in either
direction, tied to the four-year halving rhythm. A drawdown of that size
is a normal, expected feature of the attractor — but Strategy's debt
maturities and dividends are fixed in dollars and do not wait for mean
reversion. The reassuring part is that this risk is front-loaded: the
same appreciation the trend implies steadily de-levers the balance
sheet.

![Chart 3 — As Bitcoin appreciates along the trend, obligations shrink relative to the reserve; the leverage ratio collapses toward irrelevance.](images/chart_leverage.png "Chart 3 — As Bitcoin appreciates along the trend, obligations shrink relative to the reserve; the leverage ratio collapses toward irrelevance.")

The mechanism of a bad trough is worth understanding so its limits are
clear. If a deep cyclical drawdown coincided with a wall of maturing
convertibles while the stock traded below NAV, Strategy could be forced
to sell Bitcoin into a falling market to meet obligations — selling that
pressures the price, compresses the premium further, and feeds on
itself. Cumulative preferred dividends can be deferred without a formal
default, which provides a shock-absorber, but persistent stress would
impair the common equity.

| **Era**              | **Approx. BTC price** | **Obligations vs. reserve**    | **Drawdown survivability**                          |
|----------------------|-----------------------|--------------------------------|-----------------------------------------------------|
| Today / near term    | \$60k–\$150k          | A meaningful slice (~20%+)     | Fragile — a deep cyclical trough is the real danger |
| Mid term             | \$300k–\$1M           | Shrinking fast (single digits) | Resilient — buffer growing                          |
| Long term (on trend) | ~\$8–10M by mid-2040s | A rounding error (\<1%)        | Effectively bulletproof on solvency                 |

In other words, Strategy faces a **"valley of death"** in the near term
and becomes progressively bulletproof *if* it survives long enough for
appreciation to bury the leverage. Management has built exactly the
tools — a cash reserve, deferrable dividends, near-zero-coupon
convertibles, and perpetual (no-maturity) preferreds — to widen that
valley and improve the odds of crossing it. Survivorship is the whole
game, and the structure is engineered to win it.

## 9. The likely endgame

If Strategy crosses the valley, the most coherent twenty-year trajectory
is a metamorphosis into a leveraged Bitcoin reserve institution — a
giant balance sheet financing a yield curve of fixed-income products.
That is essentially Michael Saylor's "Bitcoin bank" vision, and his
roughly \$13-million-per-coin long-term target is broadly
power-law-consistent. In that end state the company becomes the largest
and most trusted Bitcoin-backed credit issuer on the planet, with the
preferreds functioning as high-grade, Bitcoin-collateralized income and
the common as a lower-beta proxy on an enormous reserve.

**The constructive synthesis:** wrapping Bitcoin in real financial
infrastructure is good for Bitcoin — it widens access, deepens demand,
and turns a volatile spot asset into something retirees, institutions,
and income investors can actually hold. If the power law holds, owning
Bitcoin and owning Strategy's Bitcoin-backed credit both look strong;
the leveraged common offers the biggest upside for those willing to ride
the volatility through the valley of death. The model is bold and
genuinely risky in the near term, but it is engineered, disclosed, and —
with time and appreciation — self-reinforcing.

And the caveats remain, stated plainly. The power law is a robust
empirical regularity with a mechanistic basis, but it is an inference
from one network's history, not a guarantee: the authors themselves note
that the address-count proxy has limits and specify falsifiability
conditions under which the relation would break. Leverage cuts both
ways, and surviving the cyclical troughs is the single biggest variable
for the equity. None of this is a forecast, and none of it is investment
advice.

## Glossary of terms

Every technical term used in this article, in plain language.

**NAV (Net Asset Value)**

The value of what a company holds — for Strategy, mostly its Bitcoin.
("NAV" is technically a regulated-fund term; Strategy's filings concede
its "BTC NAV" isn't equivalent to a fund's NAV.)

**mNAV (multiple-to-NAV)**

Enterprise value ÷ Bitcoin NAV. Above 1.0 = a premium; below 1.0 = a
discount.

**Enterprise value**

The market's price tag on the whole company: market cap, plus debt, plus
preferred, minus cash.

**Premium / discount to NAV**

How much more (premium) or less (discount) the market values the company
than its underlying Bitcoin.

**Accretive vs. dilutive**

Accretive raises a per-share metric (here, Bitcoin-per-share); dilutive
lowers it. Issuing shares above NAV is accretive; below NAV, dilutive.

**BTC Yield**

Strategy's metric for the percentage growth in Bitcoin-per-share over a
period — the scoreboard for whether the flywheel is working.

**ATM offering (at-the-market)**

Selling new shares gradually into the open market at prevailing prices;
Strategy's main way of raising cash.

**Leverage**

Using borrowed money or preferred capital to control more of an asset
than your own cash could — amplifying gains and losses.

**Amplification**

Strategy's preferred term for its total leverage — convertible debt plus
preferred equity measured against the Bitcoin reserve.

**Beta**

How much one thing moves relative to another. A 5x beta to Bitcoin means
it tends to move about five times as much.

**Convertible debt (convertible note)**

A loan the lender can convert into a fixed number of shares. A bond plus
an embedded stock option; often carries a very low interest rate.

**Out of the money / in the money**

A convertible is "out of the money" when the share price is below its
conversion price (behaving like plain debt) and "in the money" when
above (holders convert to equity).

**Common stock**

Ordinary ownership. Carries votes and unlimited upside, but sits last in
line — paid only after debt and preferred.

**Preferred stock**

A hybrid between debt and common: a fixed/stated dividend paid before
common, priority over common in a wind-down, usually no votes, limited
upside (unless convertible).

**Cumulative vs. non-cumulative dividend**

A skipped cumulative dividend compounds until paid (STRF, STRC); a
skipped non-cumulative one is simply gone (STRD).

**Perpetual preferred**

A preferred share with no maturity date — it pays dividends indefinitely
rather than being repaid on a set date. A key shock-absorber, since
there is no principal due.

**Capital stack / liquidation waterfall**

The order in which claims are paid in a wind-down: bondholders first,
then preferred, then common. Higher = safer but less upside.

**Spot Bitcoin ETF**

A passive fund (e.g., IBIT, FBTC) holding actual Bitcoin and tracking
its price ~1:1, kept near NAV by a creation/redemption arbitrage
mechanism.

**Creation / redemption (arbitrage)**

The process that lets authorized participants swap ETF shares for the
underlying asset, keeping an ETF's price tightly tied to NAV. Treasury
companies lack this, so they trade at a premium or discount.

**Tracking error**

How far a fund's return drifts from the asset it aims to track. Spot
Bitcoin ETFs have very low tracking error.

**Bitcoin yield curve / reference rate**

The emerging set of Bitcoin-backed income instruments at different risk
levels; analysts speculate it could one day act as a benchmark alongside
the Treasury curve.

**Digital Asset Treasury (DAT)**

A public company whose primary strategy is holding a crypto asset (here,
Bitcoin) on its corporate balance sheet.

**Death spiral / reflexivity**

A self-reinforcing feedback loop. Downward: forced sales push price
down, worsening the balance sheet, forcing more sales. Upward: rising
price draws adoption, which (via Metcalfe) lifts price further.

**Bitcoin power law**

The empirical relation P(t) ∝ t^β with β ≈ 5.69, measured over
2010–2026. In Santostasi & Perrenod (2026) the exponent is derived as
β_A × β_M (adoption × Metcalfe), not fitted, and validated by
scale-invariance and out-of-sample tests.

**Scaling law / scale invariance**

A relationship with no preferred timescale: the same dynamics govern
price ratios over one year as over five. Bitcoin exhibits this;
conventional assets (stocks, gold) do not.

**Adoption exponent (β_A ≈ 3)**

The power governing how the active-address count grows in time, N(t) ∝
t³ — the cubic signature of epidemic spreading on a heterogeneous,
scale-free network (a "saturation wave" from hubs outward).

**Metcalfe's law (β_M ≈ 1.84)**

Network value scales with a power of the user count. Classic Metcalfe is
N²; Bitcoin's measured ~1.84 sits just below, reflecting the declining
marginal value of each new connection.

**Long-run attractor**

The stable trend a system returns to after deviations. Here the power
law is the attractor; bull-and-bust cycles are mean-reverting wobbles
(≈±0.30 dex) around it.

**Cohort cascade**

A demand feedback loop in which each tier of holder (individuals →
institutions → corporate treasuries → ETFs → sovereigns) sells to a
larger, better-capitalised next tier, ratcheting price upward over time.

**Self-organized criticality**

A property of complex systems that naturally evolve toward a critical,
scale-invariant state producing power-law behaviour — the broad class to
which this work assigns Bitcoin's price dynamics.

**Ponzi scheme**

A fraud that pays existing investors with new investors' money while
concealing the absence of real returns. Defined by deception, not by the
funding mechanism itself.
